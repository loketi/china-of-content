Whether you are walking, running or swimming, tracking your activity has become a big part of how the world does fitness.

While pedometers, chest straps and smartphones are all able to track certain metrics, the sensible all-in-one solution is a dedicated fitness tracker watch strapped to your wrist.

Naturally, a $400 smartwatch will do the best job, but if you are a little short of cash, don’t worry – as this article proves, you don’t have to spend over $50 to land yourself a very good fitness tracker.

Sure, you won’t find watches from Apple, Samsung, Garmin, or even FitBit among the following devices. Yet, for as low as seventeen bucks, you can find a surprisingly capable watch that will have you seriously questioning why anybody would pay more.

Our chart highlights seven popular budget fitness trackers, along with short reviews and their pros and cons. After this, we have prepared a guide to affordable fitness trackers to give you the ins and outs to help you make the right choice.

Shopping for a Budget Fitness Tracker Under $50

If you have browsed our chart above, you may be surprised to see how much choice there is in the entry-level fitness tracker range. However, even though budget fitness trackers come in all different designs, they all seem to do the same – or at least a similar – thing. So how do you tell them apart? In the following sections we take a look at some features you will find on budget fitness trackers, highlighting the key things you should look out for when shopping. By the end, you’ll know what kind of things you need to find the right watch for you.

Design

All fitness trackers in this entry-level range share a few common traits – they all have a strap, a screen and software to make them work! Leaving software aside for the moment, let’s take a look at the overall design.

In this budget range, there are several different styles of fitness tracker available, with many manufacturers seemingly using higher-end watches as their inspiration.

For example, some feature a sleek square face with rounded edges, not too dissimilar to an Apple Watch. Some show off a circular design, which is more reminiscent of a Garmin or Samsung device.

Be aware that when it comes to circular watches in this price range, some may have a circular frame yet actually feature a square screen within this frame. From a distance, it looks like the watch is fully circular, yet up close the square screen is visible. It’s not a bad thing although, in our opinion, this is a clear giveaway of a budget watch.

Circles and squares aside, the most affordable fitness trackers tend to come in a band form. Here the wristband and screen are roughly the same dimensions, providing a more streamlined and minimalist look.

The face shape you go for will be completely down to your preference; whether you want to give the illusion of having an Apple Watch, or just fancy a classic circular style that looks as good with a suit as it does your gym gear.

On the subject of screens, the quality will differ depending on the manufacturer and whether you are spending closer to $20 or $50.

Some will offer a standard glass screen with a basic black and white readout. Others may have an OLED or – even better – AMOLED screen, with full color and tempered glass to protect against drops, bumps and scratches.

The strap is also an important thing to consider. Many straps will be made from a lightweight and comfortable silicone or rubber; often with multiple holes to allow your skin to breathe. Most will have a simple black design, although if you are more adventurous you may want to look for trackers with different colors.

In fact, many fitness trackers offer removable straps, allowing you to replace it with a strap of a different color or texture. Keep an eye out for those watches that come with a second strap for this very reason.

Finally, be aware of whether your fitness tracker has a button or a buckle as the connector. We always prefer a buckle. Not that there is anything wrong with a button, although a buckle feels more secure – when it’s on your wrist, there is zero chance of it accidentally coming undone.

Battery

It goes without saying that every fitness tracker has a battery, which brings us to another important consideration – the battery life.

Luckily, this is one area where budget watches actually tend to outperform high-end watches. While an expensive smartwatch may require charging once every day or two, some affordable fitness trackers will last for over a week between charges – sometimes up to two or three weeks!

This is largely down to the fact that these watches have less under the hood than premium smartwatches – which may offer things like music streaming, NFC payments, and always-on displays. As a budget model is more basic in its offering, there is less for the battery to deal with.

Still, there are variations in this range, with some of the more feature-heavy trackers lasting around five days, while more minimalist watches offer up to 20 days’ worth of battery from a single charge, depending on how aggressively you use it.

If you always take your watch off and are happy charging it overnight, then a shorter battery life will be fine. If you prefer to leave the tracker on for days at a time – potentially to help track your sleep – then you will want to aim for one with a longer battery life.

Water Resistance

If leaving your watch on your wrist permanently appeals, you will obviously need something that can withstand water – unless you don’t plan to wash your hands or shower!

The good news is that pretty much all budget fitness trackers are waterproof – or at least water-resistant – to some extent. Of course, if you are expecting to go deep sea diving with your $30 smartwatch then you are probably asking too much.

However, many affordable fitness trackers will allow you to swim while wearing your watch. Others are more suited to wearing in the rain or while washing your hands.

Some of the ratings to look out for include: IP67, IP68 and 5ATM.

Starting with the least robust, an IP67 rating guarantees that the watch is protected from ‘immersion in water with a depth of up to 1 meter (3.3ft) for up to 30 minutes’.

Slightly better, IP68 protects the watch from ‘immersion in water with a depth of more than 1 meter’, although it is up to the manufacturer to specify the exact depth.

Finally, you may come across watches with a 5ATM rating – usually those pushing the limits of this sub-$50 price range.

Watches with an ATM rating are completely waterproof, with the ATM standing for ‘atmospheres’ and the preceding number representing how many meters of water it can withstand (1 being 10 meters, 2 being 20 meters, and so on). So, an 5ATM rating means the watch is waterproof to 50 meters (165ft) – a considerable depth!

While a 5ATM and IP68 rating is a green light for swimming, there is a lot of uncertainty among whether IP67 is protective enough for prolonged water exposure. Some believe IP67 allows you to swim as normal, while others argue that it is safe for short durations, while others say it’s only suitable for brief splashes.

Ultimately, if you are considering a watch that features an IP67 rating, then check the manufacturer’s information.

If the manufacturer specifies that the device is suitable for swimming, then assume that they have tested it and it works. If the worst happens and the watch becomes unusable after swimming, at least you have a valid reason to return it.

Fitness Tracking

A fitness tracker can have a great battery life and waterproof rating, but if it can’t track your activities then it won’t be much use at all!

One of the main things people want with a fitness tracker in this budget range is a pedometer – better known as a step counter. We are all told to aim for 10,000 steps every day, and using a pedometer is a lot easier than manually counting each step!

Many fitness trackers will display your step count, while also estimating the distance you have travelled, along with your estimated calorie burn.

The one thing all fitness trackers sometimes struggle with is step accuracy – and this is a big problem in the budget range. Some trackers underestimate the steps you have taken while others overestimate. Sometimes you may have barely moved from your desk, but the tracker has recorded hundreds of steps simply because you are moving your hands.

Still, by wearing the watch for your day-to-day activity, you will be able to judge a rough number of steps taken, and compare that with other days.

In addition to steps, most budget fitness trackers offer individual tracking for specific activities, all with their own unique movements. This includes activities such as running, cycling, weight lifting and swimming, as well as sports like soccer, tennis, badminton and basketball.

Again, this is the entry-level fitness tracker market, so accuracy isn’t a strong point. You aren’t going to get as much data or accuracy with a $30 smartwatch tracking your soccer activity than you would with a dedicated soccer tracking device.

GPS Tracking

GPS tracking is a useful tool to have, especially for walking, running and cycling, as it allows you to accurately record your distance, speed and other metrics while exercising outdoors.

Budget fitness watches can do a lot of things, but expecting built-in GPS is usually a step too far. You may see a budget tracker claim to have GPS tracking, although looking a little closer and you will realize that it actually offers ‘connected GPS’.

Connected GPS means that the watch uses the GPS of another device, more than likely your smartphone. This means that GPS is present on the watch, only when it is connected to a smartphone. It’s another reason why cheaper fitness trackers tend to have better battery lives than high-end watches, with GPS built in.

This feature is still useful as it allows you to check your speed, distance and other GPS-related stats by taking a quick glance at your watch while out and about instead of having to pull your phone out.

This is ideal if it’s raining or you are running, and don’t want to risk dropping your phone or getting it wet. However, if you forget your phone, your watch’s GPS capabilities will no longer function.

Heart Rate Monitoring

Another key feature offered by fitness trackers in all price ranges is heart rate monitoring – informing you how many times your heart beats every minute.

This is a very useful feature to have, as it gives you a glimpse into your exertion levels. As you advance in fitness you may wish to train in specific ‘heart rate zones’, and a heart rate monitor keeps you informed of your intensity. You will soon see whether you need to work harder or ease off while exercising.

Wrist-based heart rate monitors found on fitness trackers monitor the blood pulsing through your veins, which gives you an estimate of your heart rate.

In terms of accuracy, heart rate monitors on fitness trackers lie somewhere between the monitors you’d find built into a cardio machine like a good treadmill (not very accurate) and a chest strap monitor (very accurate).

Ultimately, you cannot rely on a sub-$50 smartwatch to offer you pinpoint accuracy when it comes to your heart rate, so if you require a more exact measurement, you should look towards a chest strap monitor.

Sleep Tracking

We all know your day-to-day life, as well as your fitness activities, are impacted by how well – or badly – you sleep.

But how do you know how well you have slept? This is where a sleep tracker comes in. By monitoring your movement through the night, it can tell you how much quality sleep you are enjoying and when you are tossing and turning.

The good news is that, unlike some high-end watches, a sleep tracker function is built into most watches in this budget range. However, the extent of what data is measured and the accuracy of this data is determined by how much emphasis the manufacturer has put into this feature.

Lifestyle Features

These days, the line between fitness trackers and smartwatches is very blurred, with fitness trackers offering an array of convenient non-fitness features. Even trackers costing well under fifty bucks will provide some features commonly associated with smartwatches.

Of course, you won’t find things like NFC payments or call making capabilities – you need to look towards higher-end Apple and Samsung watches for this kind of feature.

However, you can now expect even a budget watch to provide you with notifications for calls and text messages. You won’t be able to make calls, answer calls or reply to messages, but you will be able to see who is calling or read messages without having to dive into your pocket to retrieve your phone.

Some cheap fitness trackers will go a step further and allow for similar notifications from apps such as Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, WhatsApp and Gmail. Again, you can usually read the notification, but must reply via the app on your phone.

Notifications aside, the lifestyle features offered by an individual watch differ greatly in this affordable price range.

However, one common feature is music control. Having music with you while working out is a necessity these days and budget fitness trackers can play their part. While they won’t be able to store or stream music directly, you may be able to control the music on your phone via the watch – playing, pausing and skipping songs without having to fiddle with the phone itself.

Elsewhere you may find things like a ‘find my phone’ feature. Providing your phone is connected to your watch via Bluetooth (and that it is within a certain range), you can press a button on the watch so your phone makes a noise, helping you locate it should you misplace it.

Other watches offer things like sedentary reminders, which will remind you to get up when you have been inactive for too long!

You may also find breathing and relaxation tools, remote cameras and female health monitoring. What you find useful may determine which watch you go for, although always make sure the core features – battery life, activity tracking and water resistance – all stand up to your requirements.

Finally, don’t forget that your budget watch is also still a watch! This means you can expect it to do things a good digital watch would do – tell the time, set an alarm and use it as a stopwatch.
